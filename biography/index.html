Biography

Few bands define an entire decade quite like Oasis defined the 1990s. Emerging from the working-class streets of Manchester, England, they became the spearhead of the Britpop movement—a cultural explosion that brought guitar-driven rock back to the top of the charts with an unapologetic, swaggering attitude. At the absolute center of Oasis was the volatile, magnetic, and deeply complicated relationship between the Gallagher brothers: Noel, the masterful songwriter, and Liam, the quintessential rock-and-roll frontman.

The Rise to Superstardom (1991–1993)

The band originally formed in 1991 under the name "The Rain," featuring Liam Gallagher on vocals, Paul "Bonehead" Arthurs on guitar, Paul "Guigsy" McGuigan on bass, and Tony McCarroll on drums. When Liam’s older brother Noel returned from touring as a guitar tech for the Inspiral Carpets, he agreed to join under one strict condition: he would have total creative control and write all the songs.

Renamed Oasis, the band rehearsed relentlessly. Their big break came in May 1993 when they hitched a ride to Glasgow, barged their way onto the bill at the King Tut's Wah Wah Hut club, and blew away Alan McGee, the head of Creation Records. He signed them on the spot.

The Definitive Era (1994–1996)

What followed was one of the most meteoric rises in music history. Oasis didn't just release music; they captured the cultural zeitgeist of "Cool Britannia."

  • Definitely Maybe (1994): Their debut album became the fastest-selling debut in UK history at the time. Packed with raw, distorted anthems like Live Forever and Supersonic, it injected hope and arrogance into a music scene that had been dominated by American grunge.
  • The Battle of Britpop (1995): In August 1995, Oasis went head-to-head with their southern, middle-class rivals Blur. Blur won the initial singles chart battle, but Oasis won the war.
  • (What's the Story) Morning Glory? (1995): This second album propelled them into global superstardom. Propelled by generational anthems like Wonderwall and Don't Look Back in Anger, it sold over 22 million copies worldwide.
  • Knebworth (1996): In August 1996, Oasis played two legendary nights at Knebworth Park to 250,000 people. Remarkably, over 2.5 million people applied for tickets. It was the absolute peak of their powers.

Turbulence and Transition (1997–2008)

The massive success brought heavy partying, relentless media scrutiny, and escalating fistfights between Noel and Liam. Their highly anticipated third album, Be Here Now (1997), was an initial commercial juggernaut but was later criticized for being overly long and overproduced—a sonic reflection of the band's excessive lifestyle.

As the 1990s bled into the 2000s, the band's lineup shifted. Founding members Bonehead and Guigsy departed, replaced by Gem Archer and Andy Bell. While they never quite recaptured the cultural monopoly of the mid-90s, Oasis remained a massive global stadium draw, releasing solid, chart-topping albums like Don't Believe the Truth (2005) and Dig Out Your Soul (2008).

The Historic Reunion (2024–Present)

In a shocking twist that delighted music fans worldwide, Liam and Noel finally put their differences aside. In August 2024, the brothers announced they had reconciled and officially reformed Oasis.

They announced the Oasis Live '25 tour, which instantly became one of the most sought-after concert events in history, proving that even after a decade and a half apart, their legacy as one of rock’s ultimate heavyweights remains entirely unbroken.
